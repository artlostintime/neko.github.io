Chapter 00 · Introduction

Why Does LaTeX Exist, and Why Are You Here

An honest introduction to the most powerful document system you'll grow to simultaneously love and despise.

  /\\_/\\   
 ( o.o )   "You wanted beautiful PDFs.
  > ^ <    You signed up for compiler errors.
           Welcome to LaTeX."

What Actually Is LaTeX

LaTeX (pronounced lah-tek or lay-tek, never lay-tex unless you want every TeX nerd in a 5km radius to physically wince) is a document preparation system. It's not a word processor. You don't click buttons and drag things around.

You write code. Then you compile it. Then you get a beautiful PDF. This is the entire deal.

💀
Dark Corner
LaTeX was created by Leslie Lamport in 1984, building on Donald Knuth's TeX from 1978. It has been causing graduate students to question their life choices ever since. Your thesis is in good company.

Word vs LaTeX — The Honest Comparison

Why You Should Use It (Especially for Psych Papers)

  • APA 7 formatting is automatic. Install one package and LaTeX handles headings, spacing, title pages, running heads, reference lists — all of it.
  • Math and stats look professional. $r(28) = .42$, $p = .021$ renders perfectly. No more struggling with Word's equation editor.
  • Citations are a database, not a mess. One .bib file. Cite anywhere. Reference list auto-generates.
  • Plain text files never corrupt. Your .tex file is just text. Git-trackable, indestructible.
  • Academics take it seriously. Knowing LaTeX signals you're doing real research.
🐱
Cat Says
The first hour with LaTeX will make you want to go back to Word. The second hour, you'll be confused. By the third hour, you'll never want to use Word again. This guide will get you there in one sitting. Trust the process. The process is suffering, but it ends.

How the Workflow Actually Goes

You write:    paper.tex  +  references.bib
              ↓
You compile:  [pdflatex] → [biber] → [pdflatex] → [pdflatex]
              ↓
You get:      paper.pdf  ← beautiful, professionally typeset

(LaTeX also generates: paper.aux, paper.bbl, paper.bcf, paper.blg,
 paper.log, paper.out, paper.run.xml, paper.synctex.gz
 These are garbage files. Ignore them. Delete them when done.)
⚠️
Warning
You will get errors. Everyone gets errors. A missing { bracket can bring the whole thing down. This is not a sign you're doing it wrong — it's LaTeX's personality. Chapter 10 is your survival guide.
Chapter 01 · Basics

Your First Document

The "Hello World" that nobody warned you would generate 7 extra files.

The Minimal Working Document

Paste this into Prism and hit compile. This is the absolute minimum:

LaTeX\documentclass{article}

\begin{document}

Hello, World. I am suffering beautifully.

\end{document}

That's it. That's a document. It'll produce one page with that sentence, a page number, and nothing else.

Breaking Down Every Part

\documentclass{article}

This must be the first line of every .tex file. It declares what kind of document you're making. Options include:

ClassUse ForYou'll Use This
articleShort papers, journal articlesSometimes
reportLonger docs with chapters (theses)Later
bookActual books with chapters, partsProbably never
apa7APA 7 formatted psychology papersAlways for psych

You can pass options in square brackets:

\documentclass[12pt, a4paper]{article}
%              ↑ font size  ↑ paper size

\begin{document} and \end{document}

Everything between these two lines is your actual content. Anything before \begin{document} is the preamble — where you put settings, packages, and metadata. Anything after \end{document} is completely ignored by LaTeX.

💡
Pro Tip
Think of \begin{document} as <body> and the preamble as <head> in HTML. This is not a coincidence — both are metadata vs content.

A More Realistic Starter Document

LaTeX\documentclass[12pt, a4paper]{article}

% ── PREAMBLE ──────────────────────────────────
\usepackage[american]{babel}   % language rules
\usepackage{amsmath}           % better math
\usepackage{booktabs}          % professional tables
\usepackage{graphicx}          % insert images
\usepackage[hidelinks]{hyperref} % clickable links in PDF

\title{My First LaTeX Document}
\author{Shuvi}
\date{\today}

% ── DOCUMENT ──────────────────────────────────
\begin{document}

\maketitle  % generates the title block from above metadata

\section{Introduction}
This is where I write my introduction. LaTeX handles 
all the formatting automatically.

\section{Method}
And the method section goes here.

\end{document}

Comments in LaTeX

The % character starts a comment. Everything after it on that line is ignored by the compiler — but visible to you in the source.

This text appears in the PDF. % This comment does not appear.

% This entire line is a comment.

The result was significant. % TODO: add the actual p-value later
🐱
Cat Says
Comment liberally. Your future self at 2am will thank your present self for writing % this package handles APA citations don't remove it instead of just having a mystery line.

Chapter 02 · Text

Text and Formatting

Making words do things. Mostly the same things Word does, but with backslashes.

Font Effects

\textbf{bold text}          % Bold — use for key terms
\textit{italic text}        % Italic — use for test names, species, foreign terms
\underline{underlined}      % Underline — APA rarely uses this
\textsc{Small Caps}         % Small caps — for abbreviations
\texttt{monospace text}     % Typewriter font — for code

% Combining
\textbf{\textit{bold italic}}  % works, but check APA guidelines first
💡
APA Tip
In APA 7, psychological test names like Raven's Standard Progressive Matrices and statistical terms like t-test are italicized. Use \textit{} for these. Don't bold things randomly — APA is very opinionated about this.

Paragraphs and Spacing

% New paragraph: leave a blank line
This is the first paragraph. It says things.

This is the second paragraph. A blank line between = new paragraph.

% Force new line (rarely needed, and APA hates orphan lines)
First line.\\
Second line on same paragraph.

% Non-breaking space (use before figure/table references)
See Table~1 for descriptive statistics.
As shown in Figure~2, the correlation was weak.

Font Sizes

You'll rarely need these manually — the apa7 package handles sizes. But for general documents:

{\tiny This is tiny}           % 5pt
{\small This is small}         % 9pt  
{\normalsize This is normal}   % 10/11/12pt (your base size)
{\large This is large}         % 12pt
{\Large This is larger}        % 14pt
{\LARGE This is LARGE}         % 17pt
{\huge This is huge}           % 20pt

Coloured Text

% Add to preamble:
\usepackage{xcolor}

% In document:
This is \textcolor{red}{red text} in a sentence.
\textcolor{blue}{Blue text here.}

% Custom colours (hex)
\definecolor{myblue}{HTML}{1a73e8}
\textcolor{myblue}{Custom coloured text}
💀
Dark Corner
APA 7 does not permit coloured text in published manuscripts. You can use it for drafts and personal documents, but if you're submitting to a journal or professor expecting APA format — black text only. LaTeX's power is often used in restraint.

Lists

% Bulleted list (itemize)
\begin{itemize}
  \item First finding
  \item Second finding
  \item Third finding
\end{itemize}

% Numbered list (enumerate)
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Hypothesis 1: Intelligence will correlate positively with creativity.
  \item Hypothesis 2: The relationship will be of small-to-moderate magnitude.
\end{enumerate}

% Nested list
\begin{itemize}
  \item Cognitive measures
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Raven's SPM (intelligence)
      \item Alternate Uses Task (creativity)
    \end{itemize}
  \item Demographic data
\end{itemize}

% Custom bullet symbol
\begin{itemize}
  \item[-] Item with dash bullet
  \item[→] Item with arrow
\end{itemize}
Chapter 03 · Structure

Document Structure and Sections

How to organize a document so it looks like you knew what you were doing from the start.

Sectioning Commands

LaTeX automatically numbers sections and handles spacing. For the article class:

\section{Introduction}          % 1. Introduction
\subsection{Background}         % 1.1 Background
\subsubsection{Prior Studies}   % 1.1.1 Prior Studies
\paragraph{A detail}            % Unnumbered inline heading
\subparagraph{Even deeper}      % One more level

% For report/book class, you also get:
\chapter{Literature Review}     % Chapter 1
💡
APA Tip — The apa7 Package Maps Perfectly
In the apa7 package, \section{} = APA Level 1 heading, \subsection{} = APA Level 2, \subsubsection{} = APA Level 3. The package styles them correctly automatically. You just use the right level.

APA Heading Levels — What They Look Like

LaTeX CommandAPA LevelFormat
\section{}Level 1Centered, Bold, Title Case
\subsection{}Level 2Left-aligned, Bold, Title Case
\subsubsection{}Level 3Left-aligned, Bold Italic, Title Case
\paragraph{}Level 4Indented, Bold, ends with period.
\subparagraph{}Level 5Indented, Bold Italic, ends with period.

Table of Contents

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\tableofcontents   % Generates TOC automatically from your sections
\newpage           % Start content on next page

\section{Introduction}
...

Labels and Cross-References

This is one of LaTeX's killer features — automatic cross-referencing that updates when you move things around.

% Label something
\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}    % put the label right after the thing you're labelling

\begin{table}
\caption{Descriptive Statistics}
\label{tab:descriptives}
...
\end{table}

% Reference it anywhere in the document
As described in Section~\ref{sec:method}, participants were...
Table~\ref{tab:descriptives} presents descriptive statistics.

% \pageref gives the page number instead
See page~\pageref{sec:method} for the method.
🐱
Cat Says — Naming Convention
Use prefixes for labels so you know what they refer to: sec: for sections, tab: for tables, fig: for figures, eq: for equations. So \label{fig:scatterplot} not just \label{scatterplot}. Future you will thank present you.

Page Breaks and Spacing

\newpage          % Start a new page
\clearpage        % New page AND force all pending figures/tables to appear
\vspace{12pt}     % Add 12pt of vertical space
\vspace{1cm}      % Add 1cm of vertical space
\vfill            % Push everything below to bottom of page
\noindent         % Don't indent this paragraph
Chapter 04 · Math & Stats

Math Mode: Where LaTeX Goes From Good to God

This is the thing LaTeX was literally built for. Your stats will look better than any Word document that has ever existed.

  /\\_/\\ 
 ( >.< )   "My p-value is .042 and I
  > ω <    wrote it in LaTeX. It is art."

The Two Math Modes

LaTeX has two ways to write math:

% 1. INLINE — inside a sentence, wrap in $ $
A significant correlation was found ($r = .42$, $p < .05$).
The sample mean was $M = 24.3$ ($SD = 4.1$).

% 2. DISPLAY — its own line, centered, wrap in \[ \]
\[
r_{xy} = \frac{\sum(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}
              {\sqrt{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2 \sum(y_i-\bar{y})^2}}
\]

% 3. NUMBERED EQUATION — display with auto-number
\begin{equation}
r = \frac{n\sum xy - \sum x \sum y}
         {\sqrt{[n\sum x^2-(\sum x)^2][n\sum y^2-(\sum y)^2]}}
\label{eq:pearson}
\end{equation}

Stats You'll Write in Every Psych Paper

% Correlation
$r(28) = .42$, $p = .021$, 95\% CI $[.08, .67]$

% t-test
$t(29) = 2.14$, $p = .041$, $d = 0.78$

% F-statistic (ANOVA)
$F(2, 27) = 4.23$, $p = .025$, $\eta^2 = .24$

% Chi-square
$\chi^2(1, N = 30) = 5.24$, $p = .022$, $\phi = .42$

% Mean and SD
$M = 24.3$, $SD = 4.1$

% Regression
$R^2 = .18$, $\beta = .43$, $p = .003$

% Confidence interval
95\% CI $[2.14, 8.93]$

% Note: APA style uses no leading zero → .042 not 0.042
% In LaTeX: $p = .042$ (not $p = 0.042$)
⚠️
APA 7 Stats Rule
APA 7 says: no leading zero for values that cannot exceed 1 (p-values, correlations, effect sizes). So it's $p = .042$ not $p = 0.042$. Also italicize all statistical symbols: t, F, M, SD, r, p. In LaTeX, $ $ automatically italicizes.

Fractions, Powers, and Subscripts

% Fraction
$\frac{numerator}{denominator}$
$\frac{1}{2}$               → ½
$\frac{SS_{between}}{SS_{total}}$   → effect size formula look

% Powers (superscript) — use ^
$R^2$          → R²
$\chi^2$       → χ²
$x^{n+1}$      → x^(n+1)   (use {} for multi-char)

% Subscripts — use _
$M_{pre}$      → M_pre
$SS_{within}$  → SS_within
$x_{i}$        → x_i

% Both together
$x_i^2$        → x_i²
$M_{treatment} - M_{control}$

Greek Letters (The Ones Psychologists Actually Use)

$\alpha$    → α   (significance level, Cronbach's alpha)
$\beta$     → β   (standardized coefficient, Type II error)
$\gamma$    → γ   (gamma coefficient)
$\delta$    → δ   (Cohen's d uses this spirit)
$\eta$      → η   (eta squared — effect size)
$\mu$       → μ   (population mean)
$\nu$       → ν   (degrees of freedom sometimes)
$\sigma$    → σ   (population SD)
$\chi$      → χ   (chi-square)
$\omega$    → ω   (omega squared — effect size)
$\rho$      → ρ   (Spearman correlation)
$\phi$      → φ   (phi coefficient)
$\Sigma$    → Σ   (summation — capital = \Sigma)
$\Delta$    → Δ   (change/difference)

Useful Math Symbols

$\pm$           → ±   (plus or minus)
$\neq$          → ≠   (not equal)
$\geq$          → ≥   (greater than or equal)
$\leq$          → ≤   (less than or equal)
$\approx$       → ≈   (approximately equal)
$\infty$        → ∞   (infinity)
$\sqrt{x}$      → √x  (square root)
$\sqrt[3]{x}$   → ∛x  (cube root)
$\bar{x}$       → x̄   (mean / x-bar)
$\hat{y}$       → ŷ   (y-hat / predicted value)
$\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i$   → Σ notation
$\cdot$         → ·   (multiplication dot)

The amsmath Package — For Better Math

% Add to preamble:
\usepackage{amsmath}

% Aligned equations (great for showing steps)
\begin{align}
  SS_{total}  &= \sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2 \\
  SS_{between} &= n\sum(\bar{x}_j - \bar{x})^2 \\
  SS_{within}  &= \sum\sum(x_{ij} - \bar{x}_j)^2
\end{align}

% Unnumbered aligned (add * to suppress numbers)
\begin{align*}
  r &= \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y} \\
    &= \frac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2}\sqrt{\sum(y_i-\bar{y})^2}}
\end{align*}

% Text inside math (use \text{})
$p < .05 \text{ (two-tailed)}$
Chapter 05 · Tables

Tables: APA Style, No Vertical Lines, Suffer Accordingly

APA tables are minimalist by design. LaTeX makes them look better than anything you can produce in Word, but you do have to actually know what you're doing.

The Basic Table Structure

% Add to preamble: \usepackage{booktabs}

\begin{table}[h]                          % [h] = place Here (approximately)
  \caption{Descriptive Statistics for Study Variables}
  \label{tab:descriptives}
  \begin{tabular}{lccc}                   % 4 columns: left, center, center, center
    \toprule                              % thick top line
    Variable    & $M$   & $SD$  & Range   \\   % \\ = end of row
    \midrule                              % thin divider line
    SPM Score   & 13.40 & 2.71  & 8--18   \\
    AUT Fluency & 11.20 & 4.93  & 3--22   \\
    \bottomrule                           % thick bottom line
  \end{tabular}
  \note{SPM = Standard Progressive Matrices. AUT = Alternate Uses Task.}
\end{table}

Column Alignment Codes

CodeAlignmentUse For
lLeftVariable names, text
cCenterNumbers, statistics
rRightNumbers when aligning decimals
p{3cm}Fixed width, leftLong text that needs wrapping
⚠️
Never Use \hline in APA Tables
\hline creates thin lines everywhere and looks unprofessional. booktabs gives you \toprule, \midrule, and \bottomrule which are thicker at the top/bottom and look like actual journal tables. Always use \usepackage{booktabs} and never touch \hline.

A Real Correlation Table (APA Style)

\begin{table}[h]
  \caption{Intercorrelations Among Study Variables}
  \label{tab:correlations}
  \begin{tabular}{lccc}
    \toprule
    Variable        & 1      & 2      & 3      \\
    \midrule
    1. SPM Score    & ---    &        &        \\
    2. AUT Fluency  & .21    & ---    &        \\
    3. AUT Original & .17    & .68*** & ---    \\
    \midrule
    $M$             & 13.40  & 11.20  & 8.70   \\
    $SD$            &  2.71  &  4.93  & 3.45   \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \note{$n = 30$. 
        *** $p < .001$.}
\end{table}

Spanning Columns with \multicolumn

\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
  \toprule
                  & \multicolumn{2}{c}{Group A} & \multicolumn{2}{c}{Group B} \\
                  \cmidrule(lr){2-3} \cmidrule(lr){4-5}  % partial lines
  Variable        & $M$ & $SD$ & $M$ & $SD$ \\
  \midrule
  Intelligence    & 13.2 & 2.4 & 12.8 & 3.1 \\
  Creativity      & 11.5 & 4.2 & 12.1 & 3.9 \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}

Float Placement

The [h] after \begin{table} is a placement specifier. LaTeX decides where to put figures and tables ("floats") based on an algorithm.

SpecifierMeans
hHere (approximately where you put it in the source)
tTop of the page
bBottom of the page
pSeparate page for floats
HExactly here (requires float package — forces it)
% Add float package to preamble for [H]:
\usepackage{float}

\begin{table}[H]  % Forces table exactly here, no negotiation
...
🐱
Cat Says — APA Tables Go at the End
In APA manuscript format (man mode), tables and figures traditionally appear at the end of the document, after the references. The apa7 package handles this automatically. Don't fight it. When you switch to doc mode (for reading), tables appear inline.
Chapter 06 · Figures

Figures and Images

Putting pictures in documents — easier than you'd think, harder than it should be.

Basic Image Insertion

% Add to preamble:
\usepackage{graphicx}

% In document:
\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{scatterplot.png}
  \caption{Scatterplot of SPM scores and AUT fluency scores ($n = 30$).}
  \label{fig:scatterplot}
\end{figure}

The image file (here scatterplot.png) must be in the same folder as your .tex file. Supported formats: .png, .jpg, .pdf, .eps.

Controlling Image Size

% By proportion of text width (most flexible)
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{image.png}    % 80% of text width
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{image.png}       % full text width
\includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{image.png}    % 50% of text width

% By specific dimensions
\includegraphics[width=10cm]{image.png}
\includegraphics[height=8cm]{image.png}

% By scale factor
\includegraphics[scale=0.5]{image.png}  % half size

% Both width and height (may distort — be careful)
\includegraphics[width=8cm, height=6cm]{image.png}

Side-by-Side Figures

% Add to preamble: \usepackage{subcaption}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \begin{subfigure}[b]{0.48\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{histogram_spm.png}
    \caption{Distribution of SPM scores.}
    \label{fig:hist_spm}
  \end{subfigure}
  \hfill
  \begin{subfigure}[b]{0.48\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{histogram_aut.png}
    \caption{Distribution of AUT fluency scores.}
    \label{fig:hist_aut}
  \end{subfigure}
  \caption{Histograms of study variables showing approximately normal distributions.}
  \label{fig:histograms}
\end{figure}
💡
Pro Tip — Export from R or Python as PDF
If you're generating figures in R or Python, export them as .pdf files (vector format) rather than .png. They scale to any size without pixelating, and they look crisp at print resolution. ggsave("plot.pdf") in R, plt.savefig("plot.pdf") in Python.
Chapter 07 · Citations

Citations and Bibliography

The part that actually matters for academic survival.

  /\\_/\\ 
 ( -.- )  "I have 47 sources.
  > ♥ <   My .bib file has 47 entries.
           The reference list generated itself.
           I haven't cried once today."

The Three-File Setup

paper.tex — your document, where you write citations like \parencite{kim2005}

references.bib — your reference database, structured entries for every source

paper.pdf — the output, with formatted in-text citations and a reference list

Setting Up Citations in the Preamble

% Required packages for APA 7 citations:
\usepackage[american]{babel}
\usepackage{csquotes}
\usepackage[style=apa, backend=biber]{biblatex}
\addbibresource{references.bib}   % point to your .bib file

Citation Commands

% Parenthetical → (Kim, 2005)
\parencite{kim2005}

% Narrative → Kim (2005)
\textcite{kim2005}

% Multiple sources → (Guilford, 1967; Kim, 2005; Torrance, 1966)
\parencite{guilford1967, kim2005, torrance1966}

% With page number → (Kim, 2005, p. 42)
\parencite[p.~42]{kim2005}

% Narrative with page → Kim (2005, p. 42)
\textcite[p.~42]{kim2005}

% Suppress in-text, include in reference list only
\nocite{guilford1967}

% Include everything in .bib in reference list
\nocite{*}

In Real Sentences

The threshold hypothesis suggests that above a certain IQ level,
intelligence and creativity become largely independent
\parencite{guilford1967}.

\textcite{kim2005} conducted a meta-analysis of 21 studies and
found a weak positive correlation ($r = .17$) between IQ and
divergent thinking.

Several researchers have questioned the threshold model
\parencite{jauk2013, preckel2006, sligh2005}.

The .bib File — Your Reference Database

Create a file called references.bib in the same folder as your .tex file. Each entry has a type, a key, and fields.

Journal Article

@article{kim2005,
  author  = {Kim, Kyung Hee},
  year    = {2005},
  title   = {Can only intelligent people be creative?
             {A} meta-analysis},
  journal = {Journal of Secondary Gifted Education},
  volume  = {16},
  number  = {2--3},
  pages   = {57--66},
  doi     = {10.4219/jsge-2005-473}
}

Book

@book{guilford1967,
  author    = {Guilford, Joy Paul},
  year      = {1967},
  title     = {The nature of human intelligence},
  publisher = {McGraw-Hill}
}

Book Chapter

@incollection{kaufman2009,
  author    = {Kaufman, James C.},
  year      = {2009},
  title     = {Creativity and intelligence},
  booktitle = {The {Cambridge} handbook of creativity},
  editor    = {Kaufman, James C. and Sternberg, Robert J.},
  pages     = {39--56},
  publisher = {Cambridge University Press},
  doi       = {10.1017/CBO9780511763205.004}
}

Common Entry Types

Entry TypeUse For
@articleJournal articles
@bookWhole books
@incollectionChapters in edited books
@inproceedingsConference papers
@thesisDissertations and theses
@miscWebsites, reports, anything else
@onlineWeb sources (biblatex specific)

Critical .bib File Rules

⚠️
Capitalization — BibTeX Murders Your Titles
BibTeX automatically lowercases everything in a title except the first word. Wrap words that must stay capitalized in {}:

title = {The role of {Working Memory} in creativity}

Without the braces: "The role of working memory in creativity"
With the braces: "The role of Working Memory in creativity"

This applies to proper nouns, test names, acronyms — anything that must stay capitalized. 
% ❌ WRONG — BibTeX will lowercase "Raven's"
title = {Standardization of Raven's Progressive Matrices}

% ✅ CORRECT
title = {Standardization of {Raven's} {Progressive Matrices}}

% ❌ WRONG — ampersand breaks compilation
journal = {Psychological Science & Practice}

% ✅ CORRECT
journal = {Psychological Science \& Practice}

Generating the Reference List

% At the end of your document, before \end{document}:
\printbibliography

Compiling with Citations (Important!)

Correct compile order:
1. pdflatex paper.tex       ← generates .aux file
2. biber paper              ← processes .bib, generates .bbl
3. pdflatex paper.tex       ← incorporates citations
4. pdflatex paper.tex       ← resolves cross-references

In Prism: usually one "compile" button handles all of this.
If citations show as [?] — compile again.

The hyperref Rule

⚠️
hyperref Must Be Loaded Last
hyperref rewrites a lot of LaTeX's internal commands. If you load it before other packages, those packages might not work correctly. Always put \usepackage[hidelinks]{hyperref} as the last \usepackage line in your preamble. The hidelinks option removes the ugly coloured boxes around links.

Finding Packages

If you need to do something and don't know if a package exists: search ctan.org or Google "latex [what you want to do]". TeX StackExchange (tex.stackexchange.com) has solved every LaTeX problem that has ever existed. Every single one.

Chapter 10 · Debugging

When Everything Is on Fire

Error handling. The part of the guide you'll reread at 1am three days before submission.

  /\\_/\\ 
 ( x.x )  ! LaTeX Error: Missing $ inserted.
  > ^ <   
           I have been using LaTeX for 4 years.
           I still get this error.
           We do not ask why.

The Golden Rules of Debugging

  1. Read the error message. It gives you a line number. Go to that line.
  2. The error is often on the line before. LaTeX reports errors late.
  3. Check your braces. Every { needs a matching }.
  4. Check your environments. Every \begin{x} needs \end{x}.
  5. Check your math mode. Every $ needs a matching $.
  6. Comment out sections to isolate where the error lives.
  7. Compile often. If you compile every 10 lines, errors are easy to find. If you write 300 lines then compile, you're suffering needlessly.

💀
Dark Corner — The Overfull hbox
Overfull \hbox (X pt too wide) is a warning, not an error. LaTeX is telling you text is sticking out of the margin. It won't stop your document from compiling. For drafts, put \sloppy in the preamble to suppress it. For final submission, fix each one properly. Nobody submits perfectly hbox-free LaTeX and anyone who says they do is lying.

The present study investigated the relationship between general

%% ── INTRODUCTION ───────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

The relationship between intelligence and creativity has long 
occupied researchers in cognitive and personality psychology 
\parencite{guilford1967, kaufman2009}. The \textit{threshold 
hypothesis} proposes that while a minimum level of intelligence 
is necessary for creative achievement, above an IQ threshold 
of approximately 120, the two constructs become largely 
independent \parencite{guilford1967}. Empirical support has 
been mixed: \textcite{kim2005} reported a mean correlation of 
$r = .17$ across 21 studies, while \textcite{jauk2013} found 
threshold effects that varied by creativity criterion.

The present study extends this literature to an Indian 
undergraduate sample --- a population underrepresented in 
the creativity--intelligence literature.

\subsection{Hypotheses}

Based on prior meta-analytic evidence \parencite{kim2005}, 
it was hypothesized that:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item General intelligence (SPM score) would show a 
        significant positive correlation with creative thinking 
        (AUT fluency score).
  \item The relationship would be of small-to-moderate 
        magnitude ($r \approx .17$--$.30$), consistent with 
        prior meta-analytic findings.
\end{enumerate}

%% ── METHOD ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Method}

\subsection{Participants}

Thirty undergraduate students ($n_\text{women} = 20$, 
$n_\text{men} = 10$; $M_\text{age} = 20.4$ years, 
$SD = 1.8$, range: 18--24) were recruited from Ambedkar 
University Delhi through convenience sampling. All participants 
were enrolled in psychology courses. Informed consent was 
obtained prior to participation.

\subsection{Measures}

\subsubsection{Raven's Standard Progressive Matrices (Short Form)}

General intelligence was assessed using the Raven's Standard 
Progressive Matrices Short Form \parencite{raven1941}, an 
18-item measure of non-verbal abstract reasoning. Items 
increase in difficulty across four sets (B, C, D, E). 
Scores range from 0 to 18, with higher scores indicating 
greater reasoning ability. The short form has demonstrated 
adequate internal consistency ($\alpha = .78$; 
\citeauthor{myszkowski2018}, \citeyear{myszkowski2018}).

\subsubsection{Alternate Uses Task}

Creative thinking was assessed using the Alternate Uses Task 
\parencite[AUT;][]{guilford1967}. Participants were instructed 
to list as many unusual uses as possible for a common object 
(a brick) within 3 minutes. Responses were scored for 
\textit{fluency} (total valid responses). Two independent 
raters scored all protocols; interrater reliability was 
acceptable ($\kappa = .82$).

\subsection{Procedure}

All data were collected in individual sessions lasting 
approximately 25 minutes. The SPM Short Form was administered 
first, followed by the AUT. Sessions were conducted in a 
quiet, distraction-free room. Participants received partial 
course credit for participation.

%% ── RESULTS ────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Results}

Descriptive statistics are presented in Table~\ref{tab:descriptives}. 
SPM scores ranged from 8 to 18 ($M = 13.40$, $SD = 2.71$). AUT 
fluency scores ranged from 3 to 22 ($M = 11.20$, $SD = 4.93$). 
Both variables showed approximately normal distributions (Shapiro--Wilk: 
SPM, $W = .97$, $p = .531$; AUT, $W = .96$, $p = .388$).

A Pearson product-moment correlation was computed to examine the 
association between intelligence and creative thinking. Results 
revealed a small, statistically non-significant positive correlation, 
$r(28) = .21$, $p = .267$, 95\% CI $[-.16, .53]$. This did not 
support Hypothesis 1.

%% ── DISCUSSION ─────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}

The present study found no significant correlation between 
general intelligence and divergent creative thinking in 
undergraduate students ($r = .21$, $p = .267$). This is 
broadly consistent with the threshold hypothesis 
\parencite{guilford1967, jauk2013}, which predicts that 
above a minimum intelligence level, the two constructs 
function independently.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations warrant consideration. First, the sample 
size ($N = 30$) affords limited statistical power. A 
post-hoc power analysis indicated power $= .22$ to detect 
a small effect ($r = .17$) at $\alpha = .05$ (two-tailed). 
Detecting this effect with adequate power ($1 - \beta = .80$) 
would require $N \approx 191$. Second, the AUT measures only 
\textit{fluency} --- a limited operationalization of a 
multidimensional construct.

\subsection{Conclusions}

These findings contribute to a growing body of evidence 
suggesting a complex, context-dependent intelligence--creativity 
relationship. Future research should employ larger samples, 
multiple creativity indices, and longitudinal designs, 
particularly in South Asian undergraduate populations.
